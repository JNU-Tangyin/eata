\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Algorithmic trading has become the dominant force in modern financial markets, with automated systems executing the majority of trades on major exchanges~\cite{hendershott2011does}. The advent of deep learning has further accelerated this trend, with neural network-based strategies demonstrating impressive performance in price prediction and portfolio optimization~\cite{fischer2018deep,zhang2020deep}. However, these ``black-box'' models present a fundamental challenge: their decision-making processes remain opaque, hindering trust, regulatory compliance, and strategic refinement.

The opacity of deep learning models in finance creates several critical problems. First, financial regulators increasingly demand explainability in algorithmic trading systems---the European Union's MiFID II and the U.S. SEC's guidelines require firms to demonstrate understanding and control over their automated trading strategies~\cite{eu2014mifid}. Second, when a trading model fails, practitioners need to understand \textit{why} it failed; black-box models provide no insight into their failure modes, making it difficult to diagnose problems and implement corrective measures. Third, domain experts possess valuable market intuition that could improve trading strategies, but the inscrutability of neural networks prevents meaningful human-AI collaboration in strategy development. Finally, portfolio managers and traders are often reluctant to deploy strategies they cannot understand, limiting the practical adoption of advanced AI techniques in finance.

This paper addresses three fundamental research questions: (1) Can we discover interpretable mathematical expressions that effectively model stock price dynamics? (2) How can we leverage neural networks to guide symbolic search without sacrificing interpretability? (3) Can interpretable models achieve competitive performance compared to black-box deep learning approaches?

We propose EATA (Explainable Algorithmic Trading Agent), a framework that combines the search efficiency of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with the learning capability of neural networks to discover interpretable trading rules. Our key insight is that financial time series, despite their complexity, often exhibit patterns that can be captured by relatively simple mathematical expressions involving standard technical indicators. EATA formulates symbolic regression as a sequential decision-making problem over a context-free grammar. At each step, the agent selects a production rule to expand the current partial expression. A neural network guides this search by predicting: (1) which rules are likely to lead to good expressions (policy), (2) how accurate the final expression will be (value), and (3) how profitable the resulting trading strategy will be (profit).

Our main contributions are as follows. First, we adapt MCTS-based symbolic regression to the financial domain, incorporating domain-specific operators such as moving averages, momentum, RSI, and volatility into the grammar. Second, we propose a novel three-headed neural network (PVNet) with policy, value, and profit heads that jointly optimizes for search efficiency, prediction accuracy, and trading profitability. Third, we introduce a grammar augmentation mechanism that extracts high-quality sub-expressions from discovered formulas and adds them as new grammar rules, accelerating subsequent searches. Fourth, we develop a sliding window training scheme with expression inheritance, where the best expression tree from one time window serves as a warm start for the next, enabling efficient adaptation to evolving market conditions. Fifth, we conduct extensive experiments comparing EATA against multiple baseline methods across diverse stocks over a multi-year period, demonstrating competitive performance with full interpretability.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on algorithmic trading, symbolic regression, and explainable AI. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the technical details of EATA, including the grammar design, MCTS algorithm, neural network architecture, and training procedure. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes our experimental setup and presents results comparing EATA to baseline methods. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with a discussion of limitations and future directions.

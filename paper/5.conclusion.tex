\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented EATA (Explainable Algorithmic Trading Agent), a novel framework for discovering interpretable trading rules via neural-guided symbolic regression. Our key contributions include: (1) a three-headed neural network (PVNet) that jointly optimizes for search guidance, prediction accuracy, and trading profitability; (2) a domain-specific grammar incorporating financial operators such as moving averages, momentum, RSI, and volatility; (3) a grammar augmentation mechanism that extracts and reuses high-quality sub-expressions; and (4) a sliding window training scheme with expression inheritance for temporal adaptation. Experiments on diverse stocks over multiple years demonstrate that EATA achieves competitive performance compared to black-box deep learning methods while providing fully interpretable trading rules. The discovered expressions reveal meaningful market patterns that align with established financial intuition.

Our work has several limitations. First, MCTS-based search is computationally intensive, requiring several hours per stock, which limits applicability to high-frequency trading scenarios. Second, while our expressions are more interpretable than neural networks, complex expressions with many terms can still be challenging to understand. Third, the sliding window approach adapts to gradual changes but may struggle with sudden regime shifts. Fourth, our current implementation treats each stock independently, without considering cross-stock patterns or portfolio-level optimization.

Several directions merit further investigation. Techniques such as expression caching, parallel MCTS, and neural architecture search for PVNet could reduce computational cost. Extending EATA to discover expressions that capture cross-asset relationships would enable portfolio-level optimization. Combining discovered expressions with neural networks in an ensemble could potentially achieve both interpretability and state-of-the-art performance. Developing online learning variants that continuously update expressions as new data arrives would improve responsiveness to market changes. Incorporating causal inference techniques could help distinguish genuine predictive relationships from spurious correlations.

Interpretable trading systems have significant implications for financial markets. On the positive side, improved transparency can enhance regulatory compliance, facilitate human oversight, and enable more informed risk management; domain experts can validate and refine discovered strategies, leading to more robust trading systems. On the other hand, widely deployed interpretable strategies might become crowded trades, reducing their effectiveness, and the accessibility of effective trading rules could exacerbate market volatility if many participants adopt similar strategies. We believe the benefits of interpretability outweigh these concerns, particularly in promoting responsible AI deployment in finance.
